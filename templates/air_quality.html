PM 2.5 Data

PM 2.5 are particles in the air that measure 2.5 microns in diameter. We measure for these specifically because they are the particle types that can penetrate past your nose or throat into your lungs and cause damage to them. The EPA has regulations in place to monitor PM 2.5 measurements. In order to be in attainment, the Annual PM 2.5 average in an area must be 12 micrograms per meter cubed. In a 24 hour span this number must be below 35 micrograms per meter cubed. PM 2.5 has been linked to cardiovascular and respiratory problems.

PM 10 Data

PM 10 is a class of particulate matter in which the diameter of the particle is 10 microns in diameter. These particles typically do not penetrate deep into your lungs but do end up in your nose and throat and cause respiratory issues. PM 10 is typically less measured than PM 2.5 with less stations monitoring the activity.

EPA AQI Index

AQI is the EPA measurement of Air Quality. This is divided typically into 6 categories. These are:
Good: AQI values ranging from 0 to 50
Moderate: AQI values ranging from 51 to 100
Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups: AQI values ranging from 101 to 150
Unhealthy: AQI values ranging from 151 to 200
Very Unhealthy: AQI values ranging from 201 to 300
Hazardous AQI values 301 and higher
This data is typically a conglomerate measurement of Ground-Level Ozone, PM 2.5 and PM 10, Carbon Monoxide, Sulfur Dioxide, Nitrogen Dioxide

Nitrogen Dioxide Concentrations (NO2)

Nitrogen Dioxide is a common type of pollutant in the atmosphere. We typically refer to Nitrogen pollutants as NOX because it can take the form of NO (Nitric Oxide) or NO2 (Nitrogen Dioxide). Nitrogen is a key component in many of the chemical reactions in the atmosphere with Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) that create pollutants such as ozone. In addition, an excess of Nitrogen in the bloodstream can lead to suffocation, as it replaces Oxygen particles. This is most apparent in babies and leads to things such as blue baby syndrome

Carbon Monoxide Concentrations (CO)

Carbon Monoxide much like NOX is a commonly emitted primary pollutant that is emitted from combustion reactions. This can be from cars, power plants, fires, etc. Carbon monoxide also reacts with VOCs to create ozone and other pollutants in the atmosphere. It is also a key component of CO2 (Carbon Dioxide), the common greenhouse gas. Carbon Monoxide is very unhealthy as it restricts oxygen flow and is very dangerous for humans to inhale a lot of. This is why we have CO detectors in our households.
